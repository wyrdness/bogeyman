## Note on Sources

This documentation draws from:
- **Cross-cultural folklore studies** — Documenting universal variants
- **Developmental psychology** — Understanding childhood fears
- **Parenting literature** — Historical and modern approaches
- **Anthropological research** — Cross-cultural child-rearing practices
- **Popular culture studies** — Modern manifestations and adaptations

The Bogeyman represents:
- **Universal psychological phenomenon** — Appears independently worldwide
- **Cultural transmission** — Passes from generation to generation
- **Parental tool** — Used for behavioral enforcement
- **Childhood archetype** — Externalization of natural developmental fears
- **Evolving concept** — Adapts to contemporary contexts

The study of the Bogeyman bridges:
- Folklore and mythology
- Developmental psychology
- Parenting and education
- Cultural anthropology
- Popular culture and media studies

This makes it a uniquely rich subject for interdisciplinary research into how humans teach, learn, and manage fear across cultures.
